My Story

A journey that started with curiosity

It started simply enough — a Computer Science student at Government College University Faisalabad, staring at rows of numbers that seemed to mean nothing. Then one day, they did. A pattern emerged, a trend surfaced, and I realized: data isn't just numbers. It's a story waiting to be told.

That moment changed everything. I stopped seeing datasets as spreadsheets and started seeing them as puzzles — each one hiding insights that could actually help someone. A business owner understand why sales dropped in Q3. A hospital understand which patients needed more attention. A researcher understand what the world thinks about our changing climate.

So I went deep. I learned Python until it felt natural. I spent hours cleaning messy, real-world datasets with Pandas and NumPy — the kind that don't behave the way textbooks suggest. I built dashboards in Power BI, wrote queries in SQL, and picked up machine learning not because it's trendy, but because it genuinely solves problems I care about.

My final year project — a Climate Sentiment Classifier — brought all of this together. It wasn't just code. It was a question: what does the world actually think about climate change, in their own words? Answering that question with data felt like the most meaningful thing I'd done.

I'm still early in this journey, and that excites me more than anything.

Final Year Project

Climate Sentiment Classifier

Climate change is one of the defining challenges of our time — but what does the public actually think about it? This project set out to answer that question at scale. I built a full sentiment analysis system that processes real climate-related social media data, classifies public opinion as positive, negative, or neutral using VADER NLP, and presents the findings through a rich, interactive PyQt6 desktop dashboard. The result isn't just a model — it's a window into collective human sentiment about our planet's future.

Toolkit

The tools I think with

Over time, I've built a toolkit that lets me explore, clean, and truly understand data. But I don't think of these as skills on a résumé — they're the lenses I use to look at problems.
